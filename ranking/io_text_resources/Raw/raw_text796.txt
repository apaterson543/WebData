The Pantry co-owner Stan Singley said he had hoped he could seat more people indoors on opening day.</p><p>&#8220;I was disappointed to see that COVID numbers were up,&#8221; he said. &#8220;I was halfway anticipating us being able to maybe go to 50% on the interior.&#8221;</p><p>Restaurants in Albuquerque are anticipating similar struggles, with some not sure how they&#8217;ll survive if they can&#8217;t seat people outdoors.</p><p>Chris Medina, owner of Holy Burger near Downtown, said he&#8217;s considering hiring an architect to design a new outdoor space suited for colder weather, but he&#8217;s worried the cost could be prohibitive.</p><p>&#8220;I know it&#8217;s not going to be cheap,&#8221; Medina said. &#8220;We have six weeks before (the weather) really hits us. business they&#8217;ve mustered.</p><p>&#8220;I can&#8217;t imagine that I&#8217;m gonna want to go outdoor dining when it&#8217;s 40 degrees,&#8221; she said.</p><p>She said some eateries have
