Homeschooling &#8220;will set back a generation of children,&#8221; according to a Washington Post column. Harvard Magazine&#8217;s &#8220;Risks of Homeschooling&#8221; article quotes a professor who calls for a &#8220;presumptive ban.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>The professional education establishment actually tried to ban it 98 years ago. Then, it tried to ban private schools, too! But the Supreme Court stopped it, writing, &#8220;a child is not the mere creature of the state.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>I wish the state would remember that.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Anyway, the educator&#8217;s complaints about homeschooling &#8220;setting back a generation&#8221; are bunk.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Eleven of 14 peer-reviewed studies found homeschooling has positive effects on achievement.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>In my new video, education researcher Corey DeAngelis explains, &#8220;Children who are homeschooled get much better academic and social results than kids in government schools.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Even though they are more likely to be poor, &#8220;Homeschoolers score 30% higher on SAT tests.&#8221; They also do better in college, and they are less likely to drink or do drugs.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;Mass homeschooling during this pandemic,&#8221; says DeAngelis, &#8220;may actually be a blessing.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Debbie Dabin, a mom in Utah, is one of many parents who started homeschooling this spring and now is &#8220;definitely considering homeschooling&#8221; next year.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Dabin bought teaching materials over the internet from a company called &#8220;The Good and the Beautiful.&#8221; Her son likes the lessons better than what he got in school. &#8220;It&#8217;s great,&#8221; Dabin says. &#8220;He likes the activities; he wants to do them.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Before the pandemic, he&#8217;d told his mom he hated school.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>I hated school, too. Classes were boring. Listening to lectures is a poor way to learn, and unnecessary today.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>In addition to homeschool teaching programs, there are also free internet games that teach things like math, reading, and writing, while customizing the speed of lessons to each learner&#8217;s needs.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Sites like Education.com teach math by letting kids adjust pizza toppings.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>For older kids, YouTube channels like TED-Ed and Khan Academy offer &#8220;free educational videos from the world&#8217;s foremost experts on civics, history, mathematics,&#8221; adds DeAngelis.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;Not good enough!&#8221; say &#8220;experts.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Michael Rebell, a professor at Teachers College at Columbia University, worries that if parents homeschool, &#8220;There&#8217;s no guarantee that kids are learning democratic values, civic knowledge.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;Were they learning that in their regular schools?&#8221; I asked.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;Well &#8230; it&#8217;s in the curriculum,&#8221; he responded.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>So what? The Nation&#8217;s Report Card, the government&#8217;s biggest nationwide test, reveals that government school students don&#8217;t know much about history or civics.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>One question asked fourth graders, &#8220;Which country was the leading communist nation during the Cold War?&#8221; Only 21% answered the Soviet Union. More said France or Germany. American students did worse than if they had guessed randomly.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Another question: &#8220;America fought Hitler and Germany in which war?&#8221; More picked the Civil War than World War II.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Nevertheless, said Rebell, homeschooling is still worse because &#8220;there&#8217;s no effective regulation to know what&#8217;s going on.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;You sound like you think\xe2\x80\x94because there&#8217;s regulation, that makes something happen,&#8221; I said.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>&#8220;I do,&#8221; he replied. &#8220;Where there&#8217;s no regulation, that&#8217;s a worse situation.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>But &#8220;no regulation&#8221; is the wrong way to think about it. There is plenty of regulation. It just comes from legislators and families instead of education bureaucrats.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>If this pandemic steers more parents away from state schools, that&#8217;s probably a good thing.</p>\n\n\n\n<p>Philosopher John Stuart Mill warned: &#8220;State education is a mere contrivance for moulding people to be exactly like one another &#8230; which pleases the predominant power in the government [and] establishes a despotism over the mind.&#8221;</p>\n\n\n\n<p>A silver lining to this pandemic is that now more parents are learning about their options outside the government
