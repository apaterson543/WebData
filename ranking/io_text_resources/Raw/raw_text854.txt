Treatment Cost Most Americans?</h1>\n<div>\n<div>\n <span class="partner">\n <a  href="http://www.nytimes.com/?partner=msft_msn"  title="the new york times logo"><img alt="The New York Times logo" src="//img-s-msn-com.akamaized.net/tenant/amp/entityid/AAvpBp.img?h=17&amp;w=0&amp;m=6&amp;q=60&amp;u=t&amp;o=t&amp;l=f&amp;f=png" title="The New York Times logo" /> The New York Times</a>\n </span>\n <span>\n <span class="time">10/7/2020</span>\n         <span class="authorname-txt" \r\n              \r\n              \r\n              >\r\n                <span class="truncate" >Sarah Kliff</span>\r\n        </span>\r\n\r\n                </span>\r\n            </div>\r\n        </div>\r\n\r\n <div class="richtext"  data-aop="articlebody">\n \n <span class="storyimage fullwidth inlineimage"  data-aop="image">\n<span class="image" data-attrib="Anna Moneymaker for The New York Times" data-caption="President Trump\xe2\x80\x99s physician Dr. Sean Conley, with other medical staff members, briefing reporters outside Walter Reed National Military Medical Center on Saturday. While Mr. Trump did not have to worry about the costs of his care, many Americans who have been ill with Covid-19 face large medical bills." >\n <img alt="a group of people standing in front of a building: President Trump\xe2\x80\x99s physician Dr. Sean Conley, with other medical staff members, briefing reporters outside Walter Reed National Military Medical Center on Saturday. While Mr. Trump did not have to worry about the costs of his care, many Americans who have been ill with Covid-19 face large medical bills.\n</span>\n</span>\n<p><em>The New York Times is investigating the costs associated with testing and treatment for the coronavirus and how the pandemic is changing health care in America. You can read more about the project and </em><a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/08/03/reader-center/coronavirus-medical-bills.html"><em>submit your medical bills here</em></a><em>.</em></p><p>President Trump spent<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/10/05/us/politics/trump-leaves-hospital-coronavirus.html"> three days in the hospital</a>. He arrived and left by helicopter. And he received multiple coronavirus tests, oxygen, steroids and an experimental antibody treatment.</p><p>For someone who isn\xe2\x80\x99t president, that would cost more than $100,000 in the American health system. Patients could face significant surprise bills and medical debt even after health insurance paid its share.</p><p>The biggest financial risks would come not from the hospital stay but from the services provided elsewhere, including helicopter transit and repeated coronavirus testing.</p><p>Mr. Trump has praised the high quality of care he received at Walter Reed National Military Medical Center, and has played down the risk of the virus. \xe2\x80\x9cDon\xe2\x80\x99t be afraid of Covid,\xe2\x80\x9d Mr. Trump <a href="https://twitter.com/realDonaldTrump/status/1313186529058136070">tweeted</a> on Monday, before returning to the White House. \xe2\x80\x9cDon\xe2\x80\x99t let it dominate your life.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>Across the country, patients have struggled with both the long-term health and financial effects of contracting coronavirus. Nearly <a href="https://covidtracking.com/">half a million</a> have been hospitalized. Routine tests can result in<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/09/09/upshot/coronavirus-surprise-test-fees.html"> thousands of dollars in uncovered charges</a>; hospitalized patients have received bills upward of <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/14/nyregion/coronavirus-billing-nyc.html">$400,000</a>.</p><p>Mr. Trump did not have to worry about the costs of his care, which are covered by the federal government. Most Americans, including many who carry health coverage, <a href="https://khn.org/news/surprise-medical-bills-are-what-americans-fear-most-in-paying-for-health-care/">do worry</a> about receiving medical care they cannot afford.</p><p>For some Americans, the bills could start mounting with frequent tests. Insurers are generally required to pay for those tests when physicians order them, but not when employers do.</p><p>The Trump administration made that clear in June, when it <a href="https://www.healthaffairs.org/do/10.1377/hblog20200625.139207/full/">issued guidance</a> stating that insurers do not have to pay for \xe2\x80\x9ctesting conducted to screen for general workplace health and safety.\xe2\x80\x9d Instead, patients need to pay for that type of testing themselves. Some might be able to get free tests at public sites, and some employers may <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/22/business/employers-coronavirus-testing.html">voluntarily</a> cover the costs. Others could face significant medical debt from tests delivered at hospitals or urgent care centers.</p><p>Covid tests can be expensive. Although they typically cost $100, one emergency room in Texas has charged as much as <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/29/upshot/coronavirus-tests-unpredictable-prices.html">$6,408 for a drive-through test</a>. About 2.4 percent of coronavirus tests billed to insurers leave the patient responsible for some portion of payment, according to the health data firm Castlight. With 108 million tests performed in the United States, that could amount to millions of tests that leave patients responsible for some share of the cost.</p><p>Marta Bartan, who works as a hair colorist in Brooklyn, needed a coronavirus test to return to her job this summer. She received a $1,394 bill from the hospital running the drive-through site where she was tested.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cI was so confused,\xe2\x80\x9d said Ms. Bartan, who is contesting the bill. \xe2\x80\x9cYou go in to get a Covid test expecting it to be free. What could they have possibly charged me $1,400 for?\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>The bills for the typical American would continue at the hospital, with the routine monitoring that any patient would receive and the drugs provided in the course of care.</p><p>Remdesivir, a new coronavirus treatment created by Gilead, costs <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/29/health/coronavirus-remdesivir-gilead.html">$3,120</a> when purchased by private insurers and $2,340 with public programs like Medicare and Medicaid.</p><p>Mr. Trump also received an experimental antibody treatment from <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/10/02/health/trump-antibody-treatment.html">Regeneron</a>. It\xe2\x80\x99s currently available to clinical trial participants or to those granted a \xe2\x80\x9ccompassionate use\xe2\x80\x9d exemption. In either situation, the drug would typically be provided to the patient at no charge. This will most likely change, however, when the treatment finishes trials and hits the commercial market. These types of drugs are hard to manufacture, and other monoclonal antibodies cost thousands of dollars.</p><p>Health economists are only starting to understand the full costs of coronavirus treatment, just as scientists are mapping out how the disease works and spreads. They do have some early estimates: The median charge for a coronavirus hospitalization for a patient over 60 is $61,912, according to a claims database, <a href="https://s3.amazonaws.com/media2.fairhealth.org/brief/asset/Key%20Characteristics%20of%20COVID-19%20Patients%20-%20Profiles%20Based%20on%20Analysis%20of%20Private%20Healthcare%20Claims%20-%20A%20FAIR%20Health%20Brief.pdf">FAIR Health</a></p><p>That figure includes any medical care during the hospital stay, such as an emergency room visit that led to admission or drugs provided by the hospital.</p><p>For insured patients, that price would typically be negotiated lower by their health plan. FAIR Health estimates that the median amount paid is $31,575. That amount, <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2020/06/16/upshot/coronavirus-test-cost-varies-widely.html">like most things in American health care</a>, varies significantly from one patient to another.</p><p>In the FAIR Health data on coronavirus patients over 60, a quarter face charges less than $26,821 for their hospital stay. Another quarter face charges higher than $193,149, in part because of longer stays.</p><p>Many, but not all, health insurers have said they will not apply co-payments or deductibles to patients\xe2\x80\x99 coronavirus hospital stays, which could help shield patients from large bills.</p><p>Uninsured patients, however, could be stuck with the entire hospital charges and not receive any discounts.
