class="entry-tags clearfix"><span>TOPICS:</span><a href="https://scitechdaily.com/tag/behavioral-science/" rel="tag">Behavioral Science</a><a href="https://scitechdaily.com/tag/covid-19/" class="entry-meta">\n<span class="entry-by">By</span> <span class="entry-meta-author vcard author">Simon Fraser University</span>\n<span class="entry-meta-date updated">October 7, 2020</span>\n</p>\n<div class="entry-content clearfix">\n<p><img loading="lazy" class="aligncenter size-large wp-image-98245" src="https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store-777x518.jpg" alt="Wear Mask Store" width="777" height="518" srcset="https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store-777x518.jpg 777w, https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store-400x267.jpg 400w, https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store-768x512.jpg 768w, https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store-1536x1024.jpg 1536w, https://scitechdaily.com/images/Wear-Mask-Store.jpg 2000w" sizes="(max-width: 777px) 100vw, 777px" /></p>\n<p>A new study by Simon Fraser University (SFU) researchers has found clear evidence that wearing a mask can have a significant impact on the spread of <span data-cmtooltip="First identified in 2019 in Wuhan, China, Coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) is an infectious disease caused by severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2). It has spread globally, resulting in the 2019&ndash;20 coronavirus pandemic." class="glossaryLink ">COVID-19</span>. The researchers, from SFU&rsquo;s Department of Economics, have determined that mask mandates are associated with a 25 percent or larger weekly reduction in COVID-19 cases.</p>\n<p>The finding of their study, still in preprint and not yet peer-reviewed, conclude that mandating indoor masks nationwide in early July could have reduced the weekly number of new cases in Canada by 25 to 40 percent in mid-August, which translates into 700 to 1,100 fewer cases per week.</p>\n<p>The study analyzed the impact of mask mandates that were implemented across Ontario&rsquo;s 34 Public Health Units (PHUs) over the course of two months.</p>\n<p>Researchers compared the results of PHUs that adopted mask mandates earlier to those that adopted mandates later. They determined that, in the first few weeks after their introduction, mask mandates were associated with an average weekly reduction of 25 to 31 percent in newly diagnosed COVID-19 cases, relative to the trend in mask mandate absence, in July and August.</p>\n<p>A further Canada-wide analysis with province-level data found a significantly negative association between mask mandates and subsequent COVID-19 case growth &ndash; up to a 46 percent average reduction in weekly cases in the first several weeks after adoption.</p>\n<p>These results were supported by additional survey data that showed mask mandates increase self-reported mask usage in Canada by 30 percentage points, suggesting that the policy has a significant impact on behavior.</p>\n<p>Jointly, these results suggest that mandating indoor mask wear in public places is a powerful policy measure to slow the spread of COVID-19, with little associated economic disruption in the short term.</p>\n<p>The study also found that relaxed restrictions on businesses and gatherings (including retail, restaurants, and bars) were positively associated with subsequent COVID-19 case growth &ndash; a factor that could offset and obscure the health benefits of mask mandates.</p>\n<p>The most stringent restrictions on businesses and gatherings observed in the data were associated with a weekly decrease of 48 to 57 percent in new cases, relative to the trend in the absence of restrictions.</p>\n<p>The study authors note that while the results are significant, their sample period does not allow them to definitively say whether the effect of mask mandates persists or weakens beyond the first few weeks after implementation. However, they conclude that, combined with other policy measures, mask mandates can be a potent policy tool for slowing the spread of COVID-19.</p>\n<p>Reference: &ldquo;Face Masks, Public Policies and Slowing the Spread of COVID-19: Evidence from Canada&rdquo; by Alexander Karaivanov, Shih En Lu, Hitoshi Shigeoka, Cong Chen and Stephanie Pamplona, 25 September 2020, <em>MedRxiv</em>.<br>\n<a
