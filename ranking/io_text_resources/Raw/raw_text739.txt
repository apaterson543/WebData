Photo: Shutterstock\n</span>\n</span>\n <p>A compound found in licorice extract that is used in traditional Chinese medicine has shown potential as an antiviral to treat the new coronavirus, according to an initial study by researchers in Beijing.</p> <p>Chinese licorice, a flowering plant that is native to Asia, contains a compound called liquiritin, which the team said was found to prevent rapid replication of the new virus strain in monkey cells.</p> <p>The study, which has not been peer-reviewed, used artificial intelligence technology to speed up the research process.</p> <p>"We recommend liquiritin as a competitive candidate for treating Covid-19," the team of medical experts from Peking University and the Academy of Military Science wrote in a preprint paper on the bioRxiv website, referring to the disease caused by the virus.</p> \n <span class="storyimage fullwidth inlineimage"  data-aop="image">\n<span class="image" data-attrib="Provided by South China Morning Post" data-caption="Licorice root, containing liquiritin, is used in traditional Chinese medicine. Drugs that can calm the immune system and antibodies that can attack the virus are also being used.</p> <p>Liquiritin is usually found in the form of herbal compound licorice tablets in China, where traditional medicine has been touted by Beijing as an important treatment for Covid-19 patients " though many scientists are sceptical about its benefits.</p> <p>According to the research paper, liquiritin functions as an antioxidant and has antidepressant, neuroprotective, anti-inflammatory and therapeutic effects on patients with heart disease.</p> <p> <p>Licorice extract is also commonly used for gastrointestinal and respiratory problems, as well as widely used as a sweetener.</p> <p>The team used an artificial intelligence system to predict the efficacy of liquiritin, finding that it significantly inhibited the replication of the new virus strain, Sars-CoV-2, in Vero cells " a cell line developed from a monkey.</p> <p>They also tested the compound on mice for seven days, finding that liquiritin "didn\'t show toxicity or side effects in two independent experiments", according to the paper.</p> <p>"In summary, we suggest that liquiritin should be assessed in human patients suffering from Covid-19," the researchers wrote.</p> <p>They also suggested that the compound could potentially have "a broad and potent antiviral function on other viral pathogens" like hepatitis B and HIV.</p> <p>In mainland China, traditional Chinese medicine has been given to more than 90 per cent of Covid-19 patients as part of their treatment, but there are no statistics or evidence to prove its efficacy.</p> <div class="scmp-china-end-text"> <p>Sign up now and get a 10% discount (original price US$400) off the China AI Report 2020 by SCMP Research.
