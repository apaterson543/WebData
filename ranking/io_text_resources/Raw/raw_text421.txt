    <div class="article__body serif cleared">\n                        <p>When her Danish colleagues first suggested distributing protective cloth face masks to people in Guinea-Bissau to stem the spread of the coronavirus, Christine Benn wasn\xe2\x80\x99t so sure.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cI said, \xe2\x80\x98Yeah, that might be good, but there\xe2\x80\x99s limited data on whether face masks are actually effective,\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x9d says Benn, a global-health researcher at the University of Southern Denmark in Copenhagen, who for decades has co-led public-health campaigns in the West African country, one of the world\xe2\x80\x99s poorest.</p><p>That was in March. But by July, Benn and her team had worked out how to possibly provide some needed data on masks, and hopefully help people in Guinea-Bissau. They distributed thousands of locally produced cloth face coverings to people as part of a randomized controlled trial that might be the world\xe2\x80\x99s largest test of masks\xe2\x80\x99 effectiveness against the spread of COVID-19.</p><p>Face masks are the ubiquitous symbol of a pandemic that has sickened 35 million people and killed more than 1 million. In hospitals and other health-care facilities, the use of medical-grade masks clearly cuts down transmission of the SARS-CoV-2 virus. But for the variety of masks in use by the public, the data are messy, disparate and often hastily assembled. Add to that a divisive political discourse that included a US president disparaging their use, just <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02800-9" data-track="click" data-label="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02800-9" data-track-category="body text link">days before being diagnosed with COVID-19 himself</a>. \xe2\x80\x9cPeople looking at the evidence are understanding it differently,\xe2\x80\x9d says Baruch Fischhoff, a psychologist at Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, who specializes in public policy. \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s legitimately confusing.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>\n <aside class="recommended pull pull--left sans-serif" data-label="Related">\n  <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02800-9" data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article"><img class="recommended__image" alt="" src="//media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18448968.jpg"><h1 class="recommended__title serif">How Trump damaged science \xe2\x80\x94 and why it could take decades to recover</h1></a>\n </aside></p><p>To be clear, the science supports using masks, with recent studies suggesting that they could save lives in different ways: research shows that they cut down the chances of both transmitting and catching the coronavirus, and some studies hint that masks might reduce the severity of infection if people do contract the disease.</p><p>But being more definitive about how well they work or when to use them gets complicated. There are many types of mask, worn in a variety of environments. There are questions about people\xe2\x80\x99s willingness to wear them, or wear them properly. Even the question of what kinds of study would provide definitive proof that they work is hard to answer.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cHow good does the evidence need to be?\xe2\x80\x9d asks Fischhoff. \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s a vital question.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><h2><b>Beyond gold standards</b></h2><p>At the beginning of the pandemic, medical experts lacked good evidence on how SARS-CoV-2 spreads, and they didn\xe2\x80\x99t know enough to make strong public-health recommendations about masks.</p><p>The standard mask for use in health-care settings is the N95 respirator, which is designed to protect the wearer by filtering out 95% of airborne particles that measure 0.3 micrometres (\xc2\xb5m) and larger. As the pandemic ramped up, these respirators quickly fell into short supply. That raised the now contentious question: should members of the public bother wearing basic surgical masks or cloth masks? If so, under what conditions? \xe2\x80\x9cThose are the things we normally [sort out] in clinical trials,\xe2\x80\x9d says Kate Grabowski, an infectious-disease epidemiologist at Johns Hopkins School of Medicine in Baltimore, Maryland. \xe2\x80\x9cBut we just didn\xe2\x80\x99t have time for that.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>So, scientists have relied on observational and laboratory studies. There is also indirect evidence from other infectious diseases. \xe2\x80\x9cIf you look at any one paper \xe2\x80\x94 it\xe2\x80\x99s not a slam dunk. But, taken all together, I\xe2\x80\x99m convinced that they are working,\xe2\x80\x9d says Grabowski.</p><p>\n <aside class="recommended pull pull--left sans-serif" data-label="Related">\n  <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02058-1" data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article"><img class="recommended__image" alt="" src="//media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18163608.jpg"><h1 class="recommended__title serif">Mounting evidence suggests coronavirus is airborne \xe2\x80\x94 but health advice has not caught up</h1></a>\n </aside></p><p>Confidence in masks grew in June with news about two hair stylists in Missouri who tested positive for COVID-19<sup><a href="#ref-CR1" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">1</a></sup>. Both wore a double-layered cotton face covering or surgical mask while working. And although they passed on the infection to members of their households, their clients seem to have been spared (more than half reportedly declined free tests). Other hints of effectiveness emerged from mass gatherings. At Black Lives Matter protests in US cities, most attendees wore masks. The events did not seem to trigger spikes in infections<sup><a href="#ref-CR2" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">2</a></sup>, yet the virus ran rampant in late June at a Georgia summer camp, where children who attended were not required to wear face coverings<sup><a href="#ref-CR3" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">3</a></sup>. Caveats abound: the protests were outdoors, which poses a lower risk of COVID-19 spread, whereas the campers shared cabins at night, for example. And because many non-protesters stayed in their homes during the gatherings, that might have reduced virus transmission in the community. Nevertheless, the anecdotal evidence \xe2\x80\x9cbuilds up the picture\xe2\x80\x9d, says Theo Vos, a health-policy researcher at the University of Washington in Seattle.</p><p>More-rigorous analyses added direct evidence. A preprint study<sup><a href="#ref-CR4" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">4</a></sup> posted in early August (and not yet peer reviewed), found that weekly increases in per-capita mortality were four times lower in places where masks were the norm or recommended by the government, compared with other regions. Researchers looked at 200 countries, including Mongolia, which adopted mask use in January and, as of May, had recorded no deaths related to COVID-19. Another study<sup><a href="#ref-CR5" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">5</a></sup> looked at the effects of US state-government mandates for mask use in April and May. Researchers estimated that those reduced the growth of COVID-19 cases by up to 2 percentage points per day. They cautiously suggest that mandates might have averted as many as 450,000 cases, after controlling for other mitigation measures, such as physical distancing.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cYou don\xe2\x80\x99t have to do much math to say this is obviously a good idea,\xe2\x80\x9d says Jeremy Howard, a research scientist at the University of San Francisco in California, who is part of a team that reviewed the evidence for wearing face masks in a preprint article that has been widely circulated<sup><a href="#ref-CR6" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">6</a></sup>.</p><p>But such studies do rely on assumptions that mask mandates are being enforced and that people are wearing them correctly. Furthermore, mask use often coincides with other changes, such as limits on gatherings. As restrictions lift, further observational studies might begin to separate the impact of masks from those of other interventions, suggests Grabowski. \xe2\x80\x9cIt will become easier to see what is doing what,\xe2\x80\x9d she says.</p><p>Although scientists can\xe2\x80\x99t control many confounding variables in human populations, they can in animal studies. Researchers led by microbiologist Kwok-Yung Yuen at the University of Hong Kong housed infected and healthy hamsters in adjoining cages, with surgical-mask partitions separating some of the animals. Without a barrier, about two-thirds of the uninfected animals caught SARS-CoV-2, according to the paper<sup><a href="#ref-CR7" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">7</a></sup> published in May. But only about 25% of the animals protected by mask material got infected, and those that did were less sick than their mask-free neighbours (as measured by clinical scores and tissue changes).</p><p>The findings provide justification for the emerging consensus that mask use protects the wearer as well as other people. The work also points to another potentially game-changing idea: \xe2\x80\x9cMasking may not only protect you from infection but also from severe illness,\xe2\x80\x9d says Monica Gandhi, an infectious-disease physician at the University of California, San Francisco.</p><p>\n <aside class="recommended pull pull--left sans-serif" data-label="Related">\n  <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02762-y" data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article"><img class="recommended__image" alt="" src="//media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18434014.jpg"><h1 class="recommended__title serif">COVID has killed more than one million people. How many more will die?</h1></a>\n </aside></p><p>Gandhi co-authored a paper<sup><a href="#ref-CR8" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">8</a></sup> published in late July suggesting that masking reduces the dose of virus a wearer might receive, resulting in infections that are milder or even asymptomatic. A larger viral dose results in a more aggressive inflammatory response, she suggests.</p><p>She and her colleagues are currently analysing hospitalization rates for COVID-19 before and after mask mandates in 1,000 US counties, to determine whether the severity of disease decreased after public masking guidelines were brought in.</p><p>The idea that exposure to more virus results in a worse infection makes \xe2\x80\x9cabsolute sense\xe2\x80\x9d, says Paul Digard, a virologist at the University of Edinburgh, UK, who was not involved in the research. \xe2\x80\x9cIt\xe2\x80\x99s another argument for masks.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>Gandhi suggests another possible benefit: if more people get mild cases, that might help to enhance immunity at the population level without increasing the burden of severe illness and death. \xe2\x80\x9cAs we\xe2\x80\x99re awaiting a vaccine, could driving up rates of asymptomatic infection do good for population-level immunity?\xe2\x80\x9d she asks.</p><h2><b>Back to ballistics </b></h2><p>The masks debate is closely linked to another divisive question: how does the virus travel through the air and spread infection?</p><p>The moment a person breathes or talks, sneezes or coughs, a fine spray of liquid particles takes flight. Some are large \xe2\x80\x94 visible, even \xe2\x80\x94 and referred to as droplets; others are microscopic, and categorized as aerosols. Viruses including SARS-CoV-2 hitch rides on these particles; their size dictates their behaviour.</p><p>Droplets can shoot through the air and land on a nearby person\xe2\x80\x99s eyes, nose or mouth to cause infection. But gravity quickly pulls them down. Aerosols, by contrast, can float in the air for minutes to hours, spreading through an unventilated room like cigarette smoke.</p><figure class="figure">\n <div class="embed intensity--high">\n  <img class="figure__image" alt="Visualization of the droplet spread when an N95 mask equipped with an exhalation port is used to impede the emerging jet." data-src="//media.nature.com/lw800/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18451102.gif">\n  <noscript>\n   <img class="" alt="Visualization of the droplet spread when an N95 mask equipped with an exhalation port is used to impede the emerging jet." src="//media.nature.com/lw800/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18451102.gif">\n  </noscript>\n  <figcaption>\n   <p class="figure__caption sans-serif"><span class="mr10">Time-lapse images show how cough droplets spread from a person wearing an N95 mask that has a valve to expel exhaled air.</span><span>Credit: S. Verma <i>et al.</i>/Phys. Fluids</span></p>\n  </figcaption>\n </div>\n</figure><p>What does this imply for the ability of masks to impede COVID-19 transmission? The virus itself is only about 0.1\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m in diameter. But because viruses don\xe2\x80\x99t leave the body on their own, a mask doesn\xe2\x80\x99t need to block particles that small to be effective. More relevant are the pathogen-transporting droplets and aerosols, which range from about 0.2\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m to hundreds of micrometres across. (An average human hair has a diameter of about 80\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m.) The majority are 1\xe2\x80\x9310\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m in diameter and can linger in the air a long time, says Jose-Luis Jimenez, an environmental chemist at the University of Colorado Boulder. \xe2\x80\x9cThat is where the action is.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>Scientists are still unsure which size of particle is most important in COVID-19 transmission. Some can\xe2\x80\x99t even agree on the cut-off that should define aerosols. For the same reasons, scientists still don\xe2\x80\x99t know the major form of transmission for influenza, which has been studied for much longer.</p><p>Many believe that asymptomatic transmission is driving much of the COVID-19 pandemic, which would suggest that viruses aren\xe2\x80\x99t typically riding out on coughs or sneezes. By this reasoning, aerosols could prove to be the most important transmission vehicle. So, it is worth looking at which masks can stop aerosols.</p><h2><b>All in the fabric</b></h2><p>Even well-fitting N95 respirators fall slightly short of their 95% rating in real-world use, actually filtering out around 90% of incoming aerosols down to 0.3\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m. And, according to unpublished research, N95 masks that don\xe2\x80\x99t have exhalation valves \xe2\x80\x94 which expel unfiltered exhaled air \xe2\x80\x94 block a similar proportion of outgoing aerosols. Much less is known about surgical and cloth masks, says Kevin Fennelly, a pulmonologist at the US National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute in Bethesda, Maryland.</p><p>In a review<sup><a href="#ref-CR9" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">9</a></sup> of observational studies, an international research team estimates that surgical and comparable cloth masks are 67% effective in protecting the wearer.</p><p>In unpublished work, Linsey Marr, an environmental engineer at Virginia Tech in Blacksburg, and her colleagues found that even a cotton T-shirt can block half of inhaled aerosols and almost 80% of exhaled aerosols measuring 2\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m across. Once you get to aerosols of 4\xe2\x80\x935\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m, almost any fabric can block more than 80% in both directions, she says.</p><p>Multiple layers of fabric, she adds, are more effective, and the tighter the weave, the better. Another study<sup><a href="#ref-CR10" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">10</a></sup> found that masks with layers of different materials \xe2\x80\x94 such as cotton and silk \xe2\x80\x94 could catch aerosols more efficiently than those made from a single material.</p><p>\n <aside class="recommended pull pull--left sans-serif" data-label="Related">\n  <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02661-2" data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article"><img class="recommended__image" alt="" src="//media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18407576.jpg"><h1 class="recommended__title serif">Fast coronavirus tests: what they can and can\xe2\x80\x99t do</h1></a>\n </aside></p><p>Benn worked with Danish engineers at her university to test their two-layered cloth mask design using the same criteria as for medical-grade ventilators. They found that their mask blocked only 11\xe2\x80\x9319% of aerosols down to the 0.3\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m mark, according to Benn. But because most transmission is probably occurring through particles of at least 1\xe2\x80\x89\xc2\xb5m, according to Marr and Jimenez, the actual difference in effectiveness between N95 and other masks might not be huge.</p><p>Eric Westman, a clinical researcher at Duke University School of Medicine in Durham, North Carolina, co-authored an August study<sup><a href="#ref-CR11" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">11</a></sup> that demonstrated a method for testing mask effectiveness. His team used lasers and smartphone cameras to compare how well 14 different cloth and surgical face coverings stopped droplets while a person spoke. \xe2\x80\x9cI was reassured that a lot of the masks we use did work,\xe2\x80\x9d he says, referring to the performance of cloth and surgical masks. But thin polyester-and-spandex neck gaiters \xe2\x80\x94 stretchable scarves that can be pulled up over the mouth and nose \xe2\x80\x94 seemed to actually reduce the size of droplets being released. \xe2\x80\x9cThat could be worse than wearing nothing at all,\xe2\x80\x9d Westman says.</p><p>Some scientists advise not making too much of the finding, which was based on just one person talking. Marr and her team were among the scientists who responded with experiments of their own, finding that neck gaiters blocked most large droplets. Marr says she is writing up her results for publication.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cThere\xe2\x80\x99s a lot of information out there, but it\xe2\x80\x99s confusing to put all the lines of evidence together,\xe2\x80\x9d says Angela Rasmussen, a virologist at Columbia University\xe2\x80\x99s Mailman School of Public Health in New York City. \xe2\x80\x9cWhen it comes down to it, we still don\xe2\x80\x99t know a lot.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><h2><b>Minding human minds </b></h2><p>Questions about masks go beyond biology, epidemiology and physics. Human behaviour is core to how well masks work in the real world. \xe2\x80\x9cI don\xe2\x80\x99t want someone who is infected in a crowded area being confident while wearing one of these cloth coverings,\xe2\x80\x9d says Michael Osterholm, director of the Center for Infectious Disease Research and Policy at the University of Minnesota in Minneapolis.</p><figure class="figure">\n <div class="embed intensity--high">\n  <img class="figure__image" alt="Baseball players, one batting &amp; one catching, and umpire standing behind, wearing masks during the 1918 influenza pandemic" data-src="//media.nature.com/lw800/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18451104.jpg">\n  <noscript>\n   <img class="" alt="Baseball players, one batting &amp; one catching, and umpire standing behind, wearing masks during the 1918 influenza pandemic" src="//media.nature.com/lw800/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18451104.jpg">\n  </noscript>\n  <figcaption>\n   <p class="figure__caption sans-serif"><span class="mr10">US baseball players wore masks while playing during the 1918 influenza epidemic.</span><span>Credit: Underwood And Underwood/LIFE Images Collection/Getty</span></p>\n  </figcaption>\n </div>\n</figure><p>Perhaps fortunately, some evidence<sup><a href="#ref-CR12" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">12</a></sup> suggests that donning a face mask might drive the wearer and those around them to adhere better to other measures, such as social distancing. The masks remind them of shared responsibility, perhaps. But that requires that people wear them.</p><p>Across the United States, mask use has held steady around 50% since late July. This is a substantial increase from the 20% usage seen in March and April, according to data from the Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation at the University of Washington in Seattle (see <a href="http://go.nature.com/30n6kxv" data-track="click" data-label="http://go.nature.com/30n6kxv" data-track-category="body text link">go.nature.com/30n6kxv</a>). The institute\xe2\x80\x99s models also predicted that, as of 23 September, increasing US mask use to 95% \xe2\x80\x94 a level observed in Singapore and some other countries \xe2\x80\x94 could save nearly 100,000 lives in the period up to 1 January 2021.</p><p>\xe2\x80\x9cThere\xe2\x80\x99s a lot more we would like to know,\xe2\x80\x9d says Vos, who contributed to the analysis. \xe2\x80\x9cBut given that it is such a simple, low-cost intervention with potentially such a large impact, who would not want to use it?\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>Further confusing the public are controversial studies and mixed messages. One study<sup><a href="#ref-CR13" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">13</a></sup> in April found masks to be ineffective, but was retracted in July. Another, published in June<sup><a href="#ref-CR14" data-track="click" data-action="anchor-link" data-track-label="go to reference" data-track-category="references">14</a></sup>, supported the use of masks before dozens of scientists wrote a letter attacking its methods (see <a href="http://go.nature.com/3jpvxpt" data-track="click" data-label="http://go.nature.com/3jpvxpt" data-track-category="body text link">go.nature.com/3jpvxpt</a>). The authors are pushing back against calls for a retraction. Meanwhile, the World Health Organization (WHO) and the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) initially refrained from recommending widespread mask usage, in part because of some hesitancy about depleting supplies for health-care workers. In April, the CDC recommended that masks be worn when physical distancing isn\xe2\x80\x99t an option; the WHO followed suit in June.</p><p>There\xe2\x80\x99s been a lack of consistency among political leaders, too. US President Donald Trump voiced support for masks, but rarely wore one. He even ridiculed political rival Joe Biden for consistently using a mask \xe2\x80\x94 just days before Trump himself tested positive for the coronavirus, on 2 October. Other world leaders, including the president and prime minister of Slovakia, Zuzana \xc4\x8caputov\xc3\xa1 and Igor Matovi\xc4\x8d, sported masks early in the pandemic, reportedly to set an example for their country.</p><p>Denmark was one of the last nations to mandate face masks \xe2\x80\x94 requiring their use on public transport from 22 August. It has maintained generally good control of the virus through early stay-at-home orders, testing and contact tracing. It is also at the forefront of COVID-19 face-mask research, in the form of two large, randomly controlled trials. A research group in Denmark enrolled some 6,000 participants, asking half to use surgical face masks when going to a workplace. Although the study is completed, Thomas Benfield, a clinical researcher at the University of Copenhagen and one of the principal investigators on the trial, says that his team is not ready to share any results.</p><p>\n <aside class="recommended pull pull--left sans-serif" data-label="Related">\n  <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-020-02598-6" data-track="click" data-track-label="recommended article"><img class="recommended__image" alt="" src="//media.nature.com/w400/magazine-assets/d41586-020-02801-8/d41586-020-02801-8_18376258.jpg"><h1 class="recommended__title serif">The lasting misery of coronavirus long-haulers</h1></a>\n </aside></p><p>Benn\xe2\x80\x99s team, working independently of Benfield\xe2\x80\x99s group, is in the process of enrolling around 40,000 people in Guinea-Bissau, randomly selecting half of the households to receive bilayer cloth masks \xe2\x80\x94 two for each family member aged ten or over. The team will then follow everyone over several months to compare rates of mask use with rates of COVID-like illness. She notes that each household will receive advice on how to protect themselves from COVID-19 \xe2\x80\x94 except that those in the control group will not get information on the use of masks. The team expects to complete enrolment in November.</p><p>Several scientists say that they are excited to see the results. But others worry that such experiments are wasteful and potentially exploit a vulnerable population. \xe2\x80\x9cIf this was a gentler pathogen, it would be great,\xe2\x80\x9d says Eric Topol, director of the Scripps Research Translational Institute in La Jolla, California. \xe2\x80\x9cYou can\xe2\x80\x99t do randomized trials for everything \xe2\x80\x94 and you shouldn\xe2\x80\x99t.\xe2\x80\x9d As clinical researchers are sometimes fond of saying, parachutes have never been tested in a randomized controlled trial, either.</p><p>But Benn defends her work, explaining that people in the control group will still benefit from information about COVID-19, and they will get masks at the end of the study. Given the challenge of manufacturing and distributing the masks, \xe2\x80\x9cunder no circumstances\xe2\x80\x9d, she says, could her team have handed out enough for everyone at the study\xe2\x80\x99s outset. In fact, they had to scale back their original plans to enrol 70,000 people. She is hopeful that the trial will provide some benefits for everyone involved. \xe2\x80\x9cBut no one in the community should be worse off than if we hadn\xe2\x80\x99t done this trial,\xe2\x80\x9d she says. The resulting data, she adds, should inform the global scientific debate.</p><p>For now, Osterholm, in Minnesota, wears a mask. Yet he laments the \xe2\x80\x9clack of scientific rigour\xe2\x80\x9d that has so far been brought to the topic. \xe2\x80\x9cWe criticize people all the time in the science world for making statements without any data,\xe2\x80\x9d he says. \xe2\x80\x9cWe\xe2\x80\x99re doing a lot of the same thing here.\xe2\x80\x9d</p><p>Nevertheless, most scientists are confident that they can say something prescriptive about wearing masks. It\xe2\x80\x99s not the only solution, says Gandhi, \xe2\x80\x9cbut I think it is a profoundly important pillar of pandemic control\xe2\x80\x9d. As Digard puts it: \xe2\x80\x9cMasks work, but they are not infallible. And, therefore, keep your distance.\xe2\x80\x9d</p>\n                    </div>\n                \n                    <p><span class="emphasis">Nature</span> <span><strong>586</strong>, 186-189 (2020)</span></p>\n                    <div class="emphasis">doi: <a href="https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-020-02801-8">https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-020-02801-8</a></div>\n                <div class="anchor-link mt40" data-toggle="anchor-links"></div>\n                <div id="references" class="references" data-toggle="anchor-links-section" data-label="References" data-concertina="true">\n                    <section aria-labelledby="Bib1"><div class="serif article-section js-article-section cleared clear" id="Bib1-section"><h2 class="js-section-title section-title strong position-relative tighten-line-height background-gray-light pt20 pb6 pl0 pr20 standard-space-below small-space-above mq640-pt10 mq640-pb10 mq640-pl20 mq640-mt0 mq640-ml-20 mq640-mr-20 extend-left" id="Bib1">References</h2><div class="pl20 mq875-pl0 js-collapsible-section" id="Bib1-content"><div data-container-section="references"><ol class="clean-list ma0 standard-space-below indented-list" data-test="references-list"><li class="small-space-below border-gray-medium border-bottom-1 position-relative js-ref-item" itemprop="citation" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/Article" data-test="citation"><span class="indented-counter serif h2 tighten-line-height text-right position-absolute grade-c-hide">1.</span><p class="tiny-space-below" id="ref-CR1">Hendrix, M.
