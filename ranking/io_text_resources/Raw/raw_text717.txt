The program, which existed long before the pandemic, was generally used sparingly by hospitals when they were faced with emergencies such as hurricanes or tornadoes. It was expanded for use during the coronavirus pandemic \xe2\x80\x94 part of billions approved <a href="https://khn.org/news/amid-coronavirus-distress-wealthy-hospitals-hoard-millions/">in federal relief funds</a> for health care providers this spring.</p>   <aside id="ad-backstage-wrap" aria-label="advertisement">\n    \n\n    <div id="ad-backstage-News_Health" class="ad-backstage" data-ad-config=\'{"network":"\\/6735\\/","site":{"default":"n6735.NPR","mobile":"n6735.NPRMOBILE"},"zone":"News_Health","targets":{"testserver":"false","isPodcastEpisode":"false","storyId":"915465093","agg":["812054919","191676894","434975886","103537970"],"blog":"103537970"},"location":"backstage","deferred":false,"isBetweenContent":true,"isAggSponsorship":false,"borderClass":""}\'></div>\n\n</aside>   <p>A full repayment of a hospital\'s loan is technically due 120 days after it was received. Medicare reimburses nearly <a href="https://data.cdc.gov/Administrative/Provider-Relief-Fund-Accelerated-and-Advance-Payme/v2pi-w3up">$60 billion in payments</a> to health care providers nationwide under Medicare\'s Part A program, which makes payments to hospitals.</p>   <div id="res915479537" class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col ">\n            <div class="bucket img">\n                  <a id="featuredStackSquareImage904455215" href="https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2020/08/22/904455215/rural-hospitals-are-sinking-under-covid-19-financial-pressures"  data-metrics=\'{"category":"Story to Story","action":"Click Internal Link","label":"https:\\/\\/www.npr.org\\/sections\\/health-shots\\/2020\\/08\\/22\\/904455215\\/rural-hospitals-are-sinking-under-covid-19-financial-pressures"}\' ><img src="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/08/20/rural-hospitals-coronavirus_sq-4f984065e8d71d0138f119b8b602f2a70ded291e-s100-c15.jpg" data-original="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/08/20/rural-hospitals-coronavirus_sq-4f984065e8d71d0138f119b8b602f2a70ded291e-s100.jpg" class="img lazyOnLoad"  alt="Rural Hospitals Are Sinking Under COVID-19 Financial Pressures" loading="lazy" /></a>         <div class="bucketblock">\n                        <h3 class="slug"><a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/">Shots - The trade association <a href="https://www.aha.org/system/files/media/file/2020/07/cms-urged-to-delay-repayment-of-accelerated-payments-letter-7-29-20.pdf">sent a letter</a> to CMS in late July asking for a delay in the recoupment.</p>   <p>On Monday, the House Appropriations Committee included partial relief for all hospitals in a new government funding plan. The committee\'s proposal would extend the start of the repayment period for hospitals and the amount of time they are allowed to take to repay.</p>   <p>The <a href="https://appropriations.house.gov/sites/democrats.appropriations.house.gov/files/Continuing%20Appropriations%202021%20Extensions%202020%20Summary.pdf">continuing resolution</a> that includes this language about relief for hospitals (among many, many other things) is still being hammered out, though it does face its own deadline: It must be approved by the House and the Senate within the next nine days or the federal government faces a shutdown.</p>   <p><strong>"Still in the middle of this crisis"</strong></p>   <p>Tom Nickels, executive vice president at the AHA, says his organization appreciates the House<strong> </strong>committee\'s effort to address the loans in the new bill, but full forgiveness of the loans is still needed.</p>   <p>Sen. Jeanne Shaheen, D-N.H., has called for changes to the loan repayment period for months and said Monday "our work is far from over."</p>   <div id="res915479823" class="bucketwrap internallink insettwocolumn inset2col ">\n            <div class="bucket img">\n                  <a id="featuredStackSquareImage735392646" href="https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/2019/06/29/735392646/have-cancer-must-travel-patients-left-in-lurch-after-towns-hospital-closes"  data-metrics=\'{"category":"Story to Story","action":"Click Internal Link","label":"https:\\/\\/www.npr.org\\/sections\\/health-shots\\/2019\\/06\\/29\\/735392646\\/have-cancer-must-travel-patients-left-in-lurch-after-towns-hospital-closes"}\' ><img src="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2019/06/26/fortscottcancer2_sq-42d8ba78982a92eba696bfb80becc7210eaf89b1-s100-c15.jpg" data-original="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2019/06/26/fortscottcancer2_sq-42d8ba78982a92eba696bfb80becc7210eaf89b1-s100.jpg" class="img lazyOnLoad"  alt="Have Cancer, Must Travel: Patients Left In Lurch After Town\'s Hospital Closes" loading="lazy" /></a>         <div class="bucketblock">\n                        <h3 class="slug"><a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/health-shots/">Shots - He says his health care system took the money because "we had no other choice; it was a cash flow issue."</p>   <p>For many hospitals, Medicare payments make up 40% or more of their revenue. Not being reimbursed by Medicare would be crippling \xe2\x80\x94 akin to a household losing nearly half its income.</p>   <p>"We have no idea what we\'re going to do if we have to pay it back as quickly as they say," Wright says.</p>   <p>In rural Kentucky, hospital executive Sheila Currans says she "vacillated" for about a week or so, trying to decide whether to tap the loan program for her hospital \xe2\x80\x94 she knew it would have to be repaid and worried that could prove difficult.</p>   <p>"It was a desperate time," says Currans, chief executive of Harrison Memorial Hospital in Cynthiana, Ky. In early March her hospital was the first in the state to treat a COVID-19 patient, she says.</p>   <p>The hospital immediately quarantined dozens of staff members at the time<strong> </strong>and shut down elective procedures. Once COVID-19 was confirmed in the community, there was a "horrible fear" among residents of getting infected, Currans says, and that kept people from seeking outpatient care as well.</p>   <p>"Through March and April and most of May, I was in a complete spiral," Currans says, and by the end of April her hospital was losing millions of dollars. To cope with the pandemic, she furloughed staff and turned one wing of the hospital into a "cough clinic" to be used exclusively by patients whose symptoms suggested they might be infected with the coronavirus.</p>   <p>Currans says the hospital is still seeing cases of COVID-19 show up, but other patients are beginning to return for services, too, such as at the outpatient clinics.</p>   <p>In terms of the hospital\'s finances, "it\'s still not a wonderful time," Currans says, adding that the Medicare loan and other support from the federal government "helped us \xe2\x80\x94 at least for now \xe2\x80\x94 survive it."</p>   <p>She\'s hoping the repayment demand will be pushed back to 2021 or, perhaps, the loan will be forgiven.</p>   <p>"I know it\'s a pipe dream," Currans says.
