After seeing those two students, huddled in the cold, she told herself: "We are never closing our doors again... It\'s our moral obligation."</p>   <p>This fall, as Bahr has slowly reopened Eatonville\'s schools, she quickly welcomed her most vulnerable students back into the buildings so they could at least log onto district Wi-Fi and stream their lessons from the safety of the cafeteria.</p>   <div id="res920351732" class="bucketwrap image large">\n            <div class="imagewrap" data-crop-type="">\n    <img src="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/10/05/itjl_vt-29_slide-e5e42058a076cd8cd5be9bbb40164840916f1fef-s1100-c15.jpg" data-original="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/10/05/itjl_vt-29_slide-e5e42058a076cd8cd5be9bbb40164840916f1fef-s1100.jpg" class="img lazyOnLoad"  alt="" loading="lazy" />\n        <div class="enlarge-options">\n            <a class="enlargelink" href="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/10/05/itjl_vt-29_slide-e5e42058a076cd8cd5be9bbb40164840916f1fef-s1200.jpg">Enlarge this image</a>\n        </div>\n</div>\n<div class="credit-caption">\n    <div class="caption-wrap">\n        <div class="caption" aria-label="Image caption">\n            <p>\n                Two of Shefer\'s children recently began attending in-person classes at St. Johnsbury School two to three days a week. With that time, Shefer hopes to re-focus on finding work.\n                \n                <b class="credit" aria-label="Image credit">\n                    \n                    Ian Thomas Jansen-Lonnquist for NPR\n                    \n                </b>\n                <b class="hide-caption"><b>hide caption</b></b>\n            </p>\n        </div>\n\n\n            <b class="toggle-caption"><b>toggle caption</b></b>\n    </div>\n\n    <span class="credit" aria-label="Image credit">\n        \n        Ian Thomas Jansen-Lonnquist for NPR\n        \n    </span>\n</div>\n<div class="enlarge_measure">\n    <div class="img_wrap">\n        <img data-original="https://media.npr.org/assets/img/2020/10/05/itjl_vt-29_slide-e5e42058a076cd8cd5be9bbb40164840916f1fef-s1200.jpg" alt="" />\n    </div>\n</div>\n<div class="enlarge_html">\n    <div class="image_data">\n        <p class="caption">Two of Shefer\'s children recently began attending in-person classes at St. Johnsbury School two to three days a week. With that time, Shefer hopes to re-focus on finding work.</p>\n        <span class="credit" aria-label="Image credit">\n            \n            Ian Thomas Jansen-Lonnquist for NPR\n            \n        </span>\n    </div>\n</div>\n   </div>\n   <p>Several school leaders say the toll of closing schools haunted them this summer, as they debated how and when to reopen. They understood that, for their most at-risk kids, especially students who are homeless, closing schools put them at even greater risk \xe2\x80\x94 of falling behind academically, not eating three healthy meals a day and experiencing abuse or trauma.</p>   <p>"There is nothing equitable about distance learning for children and youth who are homeless," says Barbara Duffield, the head of <a href="https://www.schoolhouseconnection.org/about/our-team/">SchoolHouse Connection</a>, a national nonprofit that advocates for homeless youth. "The cost of keeping everyone safe is costing some children much more."</p>   <p>Even in a pandemic, Duffield says, schools must do more to serve students experiencing homelessness. It\'s not only the right thing to do, she says, it\'s required by the <a href="https://www.schoolhouseconnection.org/mckinney-vento-act/">McKinney-Vento Act</a>, a federal law that guarantees homeless children the ability to go to school, no matter where they\'re staying, what paperwork they have or whether they have a parent with them.</p>   <p>"Even though schools are closed right now, it\'s still a lifeline," Duffield says of the law. "It\'s been consistent, and it\'s not implemented everywhere well or at all."</p>   <p>That\'s another reason many districts that remain closed for the majority of kids are slowly re-opening for students who are homeless.</p>   <p>Vanessa Shefer\'s new district, in Vermont, is now open part-time. Her kids recently began attending in-person two to three days a week. With that time, Shefer hopes to re-focus on finding work and completing her certification to become an EMT, her dream job.</p>   <p>Julie White\'s granddaughter also just began in-person classes at a new school, full-time. At first, White says, the 11-year-old was scared \xe2\x80\x94 of having to make friends and of contracting COVID-19. "But not anymore," her granddaughter says. "I love the new friends I just made, and I even ran into some old ones."</p>   <p>In Flagstaff, Rebekah Lopez and her husband now send their two children and niece to a district-run learning center, where they can at least access Wi-Fi while they continue remote learning. It\'s an improvement, Lopez says, but she\'s still worried about the ground they lost.</p>   <p>"They\'re not getting the education that they need."</p>   <p>And in Cincinnati, where Freda has struggled to work and help her kids with remote learning, plans to reopen schools were <a href="https://www.cps-k12.org/news/backtoschool">recently scuttled</a> because of rising infection rates.</p>   <p>"My children are devastated," she says.
