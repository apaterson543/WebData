cases being recorded in NSW for the past 12 days, Australia\'s most populous state has never been rid of the virus.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">\n<div class="inner">\n  <h2>Key points:</h2><ul><li>NSW\'s COVID-19 testing numbers have been falling </li><li>Asymptomatic cases are being blamed for keeping the virus circulating </li><li>The three new locally acquired cases are not believed to be related</li></ul></div>\n</div><p> </p><p>Premier Gladys Berejiklian has continually warned that, despite new infections flatlining, coronavirus was still "lurking" in the community. </p><p>On Wednesday, Ms Berejiklian confirmed the end of the streak, with three locally acquired infections to be included in Thursday\'s numbers.</p><p>Testing rates have plummeted in NSW and Mary-Louise McLaws, an epidemiologist from the University of NSW, warned just because authorities aren\'t finding new cases, doesn\'t mean they don\'t exist.</p><p>"It would appear to be unusual to start getting three cases, but up to 20 per cent of all cases are asymptomatic," Professor McLaws said.</p><p>The epidemiologist said these asymptomatic carriers were likely the reason the virus could remain undetected in the community.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg full">\n<div class="inner">\n  <a href="/news/2020-10-16/coronavirus-australia-live-news-covid19-latest-national-cabinet/12772774" target="_self" title="">LIVE UPDATES: Read our blog for the latest news on the COVID-19 pandemic.</a></div>\n</div><p> </p><p>NSW Health has a target of 8,000 tests per day, but that number has dropped off, with 5,970 tests reported in the previous 24 hours.</p><p>Since July 1, NSW has averaged more than 19,000 tests per day, but in the past week the mean is just 8,457.</p><p>There have been days during the pandemic where 24-hour testing numbers have been above 30,000. </p><p>But during one 24-hour reporting period this week, fewer than 5,000 tests were carried out.</p><p>"That is potentially a third, to a half, too low," Professor McLaws said.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">\n    <a href="/news/2020-10-07/bondi-covid-testing-centre-1/12740896">\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n  \n  \n  \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n  <img href="/news/2020-10-07/bondi-covid-testing-centre-1/12740896" class="inline-caption"><strong>\n        Photo:</strong>\n       This COVID-19 testing clinic at Bondi Beach was deserted today. <span class="source">(ABC News: Brendan Esposito)</span>\n      </a></div><p> </p><p>The time it was taking people to get tested is also a concern, according to Professor McLaws.</p><p>"It is taking time to get to the clinic to get tested, often it takes people about three days," she said.</p><p>For example \xe2\x80\x94 in the week ending September 19, eight new locally acquired coronavirus cases were found in NSW.</p><p>Three were in isolation at the time of diagnosis and for at least 48 hours before developing symptoms.</p><p>Of the remaining five cases, three sought testing within two days of developing symptoms, and two of these cases had begun isolation within two days of their onset of illness.</p><p>Two cases took more than three days after developing symptoms to go into isolation.</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg right">\n<div class="inner">\n  <h2><a href="/news/2020-09-07/how-realistic-are-the-covid-19-vaccine-timelines/12636164" target="_self" title="">What\'s the real COVID-19 vaccine timeline?</a></h2><a href="/news/2020-09-07/how-realistic-are-the-covid-19-vaccine-timelines/12636164" target="_self" title=""><img alt="" class="float--none" src="/cm/lb/12738666/data/vaccine-timeline-pic-teaser-data.jpg"/> </a> <br/><a href="/news/2020-09-07/how-realistic-are-the-covid-19-vaccine-timelines/12636164" target="_self" title="">Experts warn we don\'t know enough to rely on the Government\'s estimate of a vaccine in 2021.</a></div>\n</div><p> </p><p>The alarm was raised on Tuesday, <a href="/news/2020-10-06/nsw-reaches-11-days-without-covid-19-community-transmission/12734710" target="_self" title="">when wastewater testing found traces of the virus in the Hawkesbury and south-west Sydney areas</a>.</p><p>NSW Health also described Sydney\'s declining testing numbers as "a concern", while Ms Berejiklian warned people to get used to cases popping up, until a vaccine is developed.</p><p>"In NSW where we have an open economy, where people are getting about their business, where we are hosting major events, we absolutely need the community to be vigilant," she said.</p><p>"But we also have to accept that there is always that element of risk that we need to manage."</p><p>Going for a test was especially important for people in the Hawkesbury and south-west Sydney areas, NSW Health confirmed.</p><div class="inline-content photo full">\n    <a href="/news/2020-10-07/nols_marylouisemclaws_0807/12740742">\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n\n  \n  \n  \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n    \n  <img src="https://www.abc.net.au/news/image/12653828-3x2-700x467.jpg" alt="Epidemiologist Mary-Louise McLaws calls for tower residents in large families to be moved to slow down infection rate" title="Epidemiologist Mary-Louise McLaws calls for tower residents in large families to be moved to slow down infection rate" width="700" height="467"/>\n</a><a href="/news/2020-10-07/nols_marylouisemclaws_0807/12740742" class="inline-caption"><strong>\n        Photo:</strong>\n       Epidemiologist Mary-Louise McLaws is hoping the latest outbreak is a small cluster with a linked source. <span class="source">(News Video)</span>\n      </a></div><p> </p><p>Professor McLaws said she was surprised the three locally acquired cases were not believed to be related.</p><p>"It\'s a worrying time," she said.</p><p>"I\'ll be waiting with bated breath, wondering what were the sources for each of these three cases and are they linked.</p><p>"One would hope that they\'ve been linked, it\'s surprising that they\'ve come up all at the same time.</p><p>"I\'m hoping that they\'re a small cluster with some linked source."</p><div class="inline-content wysiwyg
