Schmidt and Katie BennerOct. 7, 2020For decades, federal prosecutors have been told not to mount election fraud investigations in the final months before an election for fear they could depress voter turnout or erode confidence in the results. Now, the Justice Department has lifted that prohibition weeks before the presidential election.The move comes as President Trump and Attorney General William P. Barr have promoted a false narrative that voter fraud is rampant, potentially undermining Americans\xe2\x80\x99 faith in the election.A Justice Department lawyer in Washington said in a memo to prosecutors on Friday that they could investigate suspicions of election fraud before votes are tabulated. That reversed a decades-long policy that largely forbade aggressively conducting such inquiries during campaigns to keep their existence from becoming public and possibly \xe2\x80\x9cchilling legitimate voting and campaign activities\xe2\x80\x9d or \xe2\x80\x9cinterjecting the investigation itself as an issue\xe2\x80\x9d for voters.The memo creates \xe2\x80\x9can exception to the general non-interference with elections policy\xe2\x80\x9d for suspicions of election fraud, particularly misconduct by federal government workers, including postal workers or military employees; both groups transport mail-in ballots. The exception allows investigators to take overt investigative steps, like questioning witnesses, that were previously off limits in such inquiries until after election results were certified.The move also allows prosecutors to make more of a spectacle of election fraud in the weeks before the vote on Nov. 3.
