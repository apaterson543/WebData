(Arash Ashourinia/AP)Opinion by Editorial BoardOctober 6, 2020 at 6:05 PM EDTIRAN, ONE of the countries hardest hit by the coronavirus, has imposed another lockdown on its sprawling capital, Tehran, shutting schools, cafes, movie theaters, hair salons and even mosques. The measure was needed: On Monday, 3,900 new infections and 235 deaths were reported \xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x94 the most since July. Yet the regime of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei has failed to take a simple measure to contain the virus and spare innocent human lives: releasing lawyers, journalists, human rights activists and Western nationals it has imprisoned for political reasons.Support our journalism. Subscribe today.arrow-rightSince February, some 120,000 inmates have been temporarily released from Iran\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x99s squalid and overcrowded prisons, according to the United Nations. But that has not included prominent political prisoners. On the contrary, said U.N. human rights chief Michelle Bachelet in a statement Tuesday, \xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9cmeasures designed to mitigate the spread of COVID-19 have been used in a discriminatory way against this specific group of prisoners.\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9dThe case of Nasrin Sotoudeh, a 57-year-old human rights lawyer, exemplifies that cruelty. Ms. Sotoudeh was arrested in 2018 and sentenced a year later to 38 years in prison and 148 lashes. Her crime? Defending women who peacefully protested Iran\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x99s compulsory head-covering law by removing their hijabs in public. It was the second time the lawyer had been handed a term in the notorious Evin prison; she served time from 2010 to 2013 for defending human rights activists, including the Nobel Prize-winning Shirin Ebadi.ADADIn August, Ms. Sotoudeh launched a hunger strike to protest the regime\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x99s potentially lethal discrimination against political detainees. After more than five weeks of consuming only water, tea, sugar and salt, she was hospitalized on Sept. 19, reportedly with a severe heart condition. Yet only four days later, she was returned to her cell at Evin, where she has remained. Her husband said she ended her hunger strike on Sept. 26 as her health continued to deteriorate.Ms. Sotoudeh\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x99s treatment prompted a statement from 16 U.N. special rapporteurs and other human rights experts, who said \xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9cit is unfathomable that the Iranian authorities would return Ms. Sotoudeh to prison where she is at heightened risk to COVID-19, as well as with her serious heart condition.\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9dUnfathomable, perhaps, but not unprecedented. The Islamist regime has a long history of vicious repression of peaceful dissent. Even the humanitarian catastrophe of a global pandemic that has killed more than 27,000 of its citizens has not tempered its brutality. The regime is trying to portray itself to governments in Europe and elsewhere in the world as a victim of U.S. aggression; it blames the Trump administration\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x99s \xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9cmaximum pressure\xc3\xa2\xc2\x80\xc2\x9d campaign for its poor response to the pandemic.
